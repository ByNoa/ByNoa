Elizia Font

Typography based on Elizabeth I signature
2025

Typography, Editorial

Elizia is a display typeface inspired by the Queen Elizabeth I’s signature. I translated her powerful and ornamental handwriting into a contemporary typographic form. This project aims to reinterpret historical authority through design, strength and elegance. Designed for cultural, editorial and fashion contexts. Elizia works best in headlines, capital letters and decorative applications. Its calligraphic style can coexist with classic serif typefaces such as Baskerville or Bodoni. This font was created using Procreate, Illustrator and Glyphs.

  • Problem

    Contemporary fonts are standardized, optimized and replicated. In a digital environment dominated by copy-paste systems and industrial production, written language often loses its emotional weight, individuality and sense of authorship.

  • Insight

    There is a growing nostalgia for pre-digital forms of expression, where writing was imperfect, personal and deeply human. Calligraphy carried authority, intention and identity. Those qualities contrast with today’s automatization systems.

  • Solution

    Elizia is a typeface inspired by Elizabeth I’s signature, chosen as a reference for its historical meaning and expressive complexity. Her handwriting is one of the earliest written forms to symbolize power, independence and authorship for women. Today it is still studied in calligraphy classes due to its technical and gestural richness. Elizia reintroduces imperfection, character, personality and emotional depth into a digital font, offering an alternative to industrialized uniformity.

More font applications

This display typeface works as ornamentation, capital letter or headline. It can be followed by serif fonts. In this case, it coexist with Brioso. Elizia font has been used in several Sofia Coppola and Jane Austen’s movie posters. These two figures were chosen because they share a common atmosphere and they also show what strength in feminity in different scenarios would be like. This goes hand in hand with the font’s purpose.
